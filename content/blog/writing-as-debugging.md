I started keeping notes about the things I build because I kept forgetting them. Not the high-level decisions — those stick — but the specific reasons. Why did I choose this approach over the obvious one? What constraint was I working around? What did I try first and why did it fail?

## The debugging analogy

When you debug code, you form a hypothesis, test it, and update your model of the system. Writing does the same thing for ideas. The act of explaining something — to a hypothetical reader, to future-you — forces you to test whether you actually understand it.

Gaps appear when you write that don't appear when you think. You'll reach for a word and realize you're not sure what the word should be. That uncertainty is information.

## Background

I had a blog from 2017 to 2020 that I abandoned. The posts were long, infrequent, and tried too hard to be comprehensive. I wrote them like documentation, not like notes. They took days and felt like obligations.

## Approach

This iteration is different by design:

- Posts can be short — 4 minutes of reading is enough if the idea is complete
- I write them the week I'm thinking about the thing, not months later from memory
- I aim to leave one thing out of each post — the detail that would take three paragraphs to explain and isn't load-bearing for the main point

### Trade-offs

- Short posts get less SEO surface area — I don't care
- Writing while you're close to a problem means less perspective — the post captures a moment, not the whole arc; that's fine

## Outcome

I've published nine posts in the last four months. The act of writing them has, at least twice, revealed a flaw in my approach before I shipped it.

That's the real value. Not the posts themselves.
